#Flash 3.5
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is an AI technique proposed in 2020 that enhances large language models (LLMs) by integrating an external information-retrieval mechanism. Instead of relying solely on static training data, RAG allows LLMs to access domain-specific or updated information from external databases, web sources, and uploaded documents. This process supplements the model's pre-existing knowledge, reduces computational and financial costs associated with retraining, and lowers the frequency of AI hallucinations. It also introduces transparency by allowing models to cite verifiable sources.

The RAG process converts data into numerical embeddings stored in a vector database. When a user submits a query, a document retriever selects relevant documents to augment the prompt, encouraging the LLM to prioritize the new data. Improvements to RAG can be applied across various stages, including encoder optimizations, retriever-centric methods, model redesigns like Retro, distinct chunking strategies, and hybrid searches that combine semantic and traditional text searches. Despite these advancements, challenges remain. RAG does not completely eliminate hallucinations, as LLMs can still misinterpret contexts, struggle with conflicting information, merge outdated data, or generate responses when they actually lack sufficient information.
