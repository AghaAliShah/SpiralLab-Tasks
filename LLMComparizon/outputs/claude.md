#Haiku
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a technique that enhances large language models by enabling them to retrieve and incorporate information from external data sources rather than relying solely on training data. Introduced in 2020, RAG has become widely adopted in modern AI systems. The approach improves LLM performance by reducing hallucinations and eliminating the need to retrain models with new information, instead augmenting external knowledge bases when updates occur. RAG also enables systems to cite sources, providing greater transparency for users to verify accuracy.

The RAG process typically converts reference data into embeddings stored in vector databases. When users submit queries, a retriever selects relevant documents that are then incorporated into prompts fed to the LLM for generation. Various enhancements can optimize this process through improved encoding methods, retriever optimization, chunking strategies, and hybrid search approaches.

However, RAG has significant limitations. It does not prevent all hallucinations, as LLMs can still misinterpret retrieved information or combine details from conflicting sources misleadingly—a phenomenon called RAG poisoning. Additionally, models may generate answers despite lacking sufficient information to do so reliably, and they sometimes struggle to recognize context within retrieved sources.
